How I work

How I take a fuzzy problem and turn it into a shipped product.

Every project I work on moves through the same five stages. The order matters more than the ceremony: I try to get the problem right before I open a design tool, and I keep paying attention after we ship. I lean on AI for the heavy lifting early on, reading through research, sorting signal from noise, sketching options fast, so more of my time goes to judgment and craft.

  1. 01

    Frame

    Get the problem right before I design anything.

    I try to understand a problem well enough to explain it in a sentence before I touch a design tool. An LLM is a good thinking partner here: I feed it the research, the tickets, and the half-formed brief, then push on it until I can say who this is for, what is actually in the way, and what we should not bother building. Skip this and you spend weeks polishing the wrong thing.

    • Read the research
    • Sort signal from noise
    • Name the real problem
    • Decide what to skip

    What I walk away withA problem I can state in one sentence, and a clear reason it is worth solving.

  2. 02

    Validate

    Check it against real people, early.

    I would rather be wrong early and cheaply than late and expensively. So before I commit to a direction, I talk to users, sit in on support calls, read tickets, and look at how people actually behave in the product. Most of my first assumptions do not survive that, which is exactly the point. The same few frustrations tend to show up again and again, and those are what I design against.

    • Customer interviews
    • Usability sessions
    • Support tickets
    • Behavioral data

    What I walk away withThe riskiest assumptions get tested while they are still cheap to change.

  3. 03

    Prototype

    Build something people can actually use.

    A static mockup hides how something feels once you tap it, wait on it, or hit an error. So I build prototypes the team can use for real, with the actual states, transitions, and the ways things break. It is easier to react to a working thing than to a picture of one, and AI tools let me get there fast enough that the prototype is cheap to throw away and redo.

    • Interaction design
    • Real states
    • Transitions
    • Failure paths

    What I walk away withBehavior settled in something real, before the expensive build starts.

  4. 04

    Systemize

    Turn it into something a team can build.

    Once a direction holds up, I make it something a team can build without guessing: clear hierarchy, components that get reused, accessible defaults, and specs detailed enough that engineering is not left filling in the gaps. Figma is where I tighten and document the work, not where I invent it. By this point the hard calls are already made.

    • Hierarchy
    • Components
    • Accessible defaults
    • Handoff specs

    What I walk away withA consistent, buildable design that holds up well past the first screen.

  5. 05

    Iterate

    Ship, watch what happens, then keep going.

    Launch is where I learn the most. I watch what people actually do once it is live, in analytics, session recordings, and the next round of tickets, and I feed that straight back into the work. Nothing I ship is final. It is just the current best guess, and there is almost always a clearer version waiting in what real usage tells me.

    • Usability testing
    • Analytics
    • Session recordings
    • Next-round tickets

    What I walk away withEach release is shaped by the last, based on behavior instead of opinion.
